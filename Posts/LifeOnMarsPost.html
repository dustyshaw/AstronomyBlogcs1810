Life on Mars

In the Fall of 1964, the Mariner 4 frantically captured it 6-bit images of Mars. With just 310 Watts of solar power (just enough to power one vaccuum cleaner) the Mariner 4 sent those images back to Earth from deep space. However, the first ever close-up images of Mars showed something scientists didn`t expect: Earths closest cousin in our solar system seemed to havecraters.

Mars water

While Mars does contain water, most of it is in a permafrost, a layer of ice that doesn`t melt throughout the year. You can also see ice caps on Mars. These ice caps are much like the Earth in that they do not melt thorughout the season. We have learned from our rovers that there might be a layer of water casted in pockets of rock.

This water may or may not be usable. We would need to create complex systems of water management.
